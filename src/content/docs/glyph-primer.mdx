title: Glyph primer
description: What glyph is, why any model can read it, and how to think about it in three minutes.
---

Glyph is APE's internal notation. It's **translation, not compression** —
every name, date, place, and number survives. What disappears is English
filler. What replaces it is **operators**: symbols borrowed from math,
logic, and set theory that encode relationships directly.

```
(day:Tue_standup)Priya→flagged:auth_bug=P0>blocks:launch
```

*On Tuesday's standup, Priya flagged the auth bug — a P0 that blocks launch.*

## Any model can read it

This is the feature that matters most.

Glyph is not a specification you have to teach a model. It's an **expression
language** — its operators are symbols whose meanings are already common
property. `→` means flow. `¬` means not. `⊂` means subset. `∃` means
"there exists." A pretrained LLM that has never seen a line of glyph still
reads `crash→startup|trigger:v2.4_update` and understands it — the same way
you read an equation in a paper you've never cracked. The operators are
self-evidencing.

That portability is the whole point. Memories written as glyph today are
readable by the model you use tomorrow, the one after that, and the
open-weight successor you self-host when a vendor pivots. Your cortex
outlives the model.

## The operator set is open

There's no fixed alphabet. The translator reaches for the most precise
operator for each relationship and coins a new one when nothing existing
fits. A taste of what glyph leans on:

| Category  | Operators                                                                               |
|-----------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Flow      | `→` causes · `↔` reciprocal · `⇒` implies · `Δ` changes                                 |
| Equality  | `:` property · `=` equals · `≡` identical · `≈` approximately                           |
| Logic     | `¬` not · `∧` and · `∨` or · `⊂` subset · `∃` exists · `∀` for all · `⊥` undefined      |
| Structure | `( )` qualifier · `[a\|b]` bundle · `=N:[…]` cardinality · `a..b` range                 |
| Glue      | `_` joins tokens inside a word · `\|` marks parallel items                              |

## Two rules

1. **No spaces, no filler.** Use `_` to join words inside a token. Drop
   articles and copulas (*the, is, are, with, and, or, for…*).
2. **Preserve the real stuff.** Names, dates, numbers, identifiers —
   verbatim.

Everything else follows from picking the most precise operator available.

## Two from the corpus

Short examples tell you the rules. These two show the reach. Both are
real records from the corpus APE's boutique models trained on — the prose
on top is the human source, the glyph below is what the translator
learned to produce.

### The halting problem

> The halting problem is a fundamental theorem in computability theory,
> proven by Alan Turing in 1936. It demonstrates that there is no general
> algorithm capable of determining whether an arbitrary computer program
> will eventually finish running or continue forever. This undecidability
> is established through a proof by contradiction involving a
> self-referential machine. If such a decider existed, one could construct
> a machine that halts if and only if it does not halt, creating a logical
> paradox. Consequently, the problem defines the absolute boundaries of
> what can be computed by any Turing machine.

Becomes:

```
halting_problem(computability_theory)⊂theorem
proven_by:Alan_Turing(1936)
∃[decider(arbitrary_program)→{finishes|forever}]⇒¬∃(general_algorithm)
proof:[contradiction(self-referential_machine)]→[if(decider)⇒exists(machine):halts↔¬halts]→paradox
halting_problem=boundaries(computable_by:Turing_machine)
```

Notice `halts↔¬halts` — the paradox itself, rendered as a single
biconditional between an assertion and its negation. Five lines carry a
theorem, its author, its proof structure, and its consequence.

### The French Revolution

> The French Revolution (1789-1799) wasn't a spontaneous eruption, but the
> culmination of decades of systemic inequality. Decades of lavish royal
> spending, coupled with costly wars (like supporting the American
> Revolution), had drained the national treasury. This, combined with a
> rigid feudal system where the Third Estate (commoners) bore the brunt of
> taxation while the nobility and clergy enjoyed exemptions, fostered deep
> resentment. Enlightenment ideals — emphasizing reason, individual rights,
> and popular sovereignty — provided a philosophical framework for
> challenging the status quo. The immediate trigger was a severe famine in
> 1788, leading to widespread bread riots. This sparked the storming of
> the Bastille, escalating into a revolution that ultimately toppled the
> monarchy, but devolved into the Reign of Terror, highlighting the
> dangers of unchecked revolutionary fervor.

Becomes:

```
French_Revolution(1789..1799)={[royal_spending|costly_wars(American_Revolution)]→national_treasury(drain)∧rigid_feudal_system(Third_Estate(taxation_burden)↔[nobility|clergy](exemptions)⇒resentment)|Enlightenment_ideals(reason|individual_rights|popular_sovereignty)→philosophical_framework}∧famine(1788)→bread_riots→storming_of_the_Bastille→revolution(topple_monarchy)→Reign_of_Terror(unchecked_revolutionary_fervor)
```

Notice `1789..1799` — a decade as a range operator — and the final causal
chain, `storming_of_the_Bastille → revolution(topple_monarchy) → Reign_of_Terror`.
A decade of history folded into one expression.
